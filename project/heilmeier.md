1. What are you trying to do? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.

I am building a highly efficient image-recognition system for remote, battery-powered wildlife trail cameras using a Binary Neural Network (BNN) algorithm. My objective is to create a specialized microchip that acts as a continuous, ultra-low-power smart filter, simply asking: "Is there an animal in this photo, or is it empty?" By specifically accelerating the network's BinarizeConv2d kernel in hardware, it can perform this check instantly with very little energy. This allows the camera to actively scan for all types of wildlife—including cold-blooded reptiles that defeat traditional heat sensors—while running continuously for months on a small solar panel without an internet connection.

2. How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?

Today, adding advanced image recognition to a remote camera requires using a standard computer processor. The limit of current practice is that these general-purpose processors are designed for highly complex, high-precision math. Our specific image-recognition algorithm has been simplified to only use 1s and 0s, but standard processors cannot process data this way; they force our simple 1-bit data into heavy 32-bit formats.

This creates a massive data-traffic jam. In our software baseline tests on an Apple M1 processor, the system was severely restricted by memory bandwidth, spending most of its time waiting for data and utilizing only 3.2% of its compute capability. Because this inefficiency drains batteries so rapidly, current AI cameras are forced to sleep and rely on Passive Infrared (PIR) heat sensors to wake them up. This is a severe limitation: heat sensors routinely miss cold-blooded animals, fail on fast-moving predators, and drain batteries by waking the processor for false alarms like swaying branches.

3. What is new in your approach and why do you think it will be successful?

Instead of forcing a general-purpose CPU to do the work, I am moving the heaviest part of the workload—three binary convolution layers representing 94% of total compute—onto a custom hardware accelerator. This custom hardware physically stores and processes the data in its native 1-bit format, replacing power-hungry math processors with simple, highly efficient XNOR logic gates. The first convolution layer is intentionally retained at 8-bit precision on the host processor, preserving the spatial detail needed to achieve high accuracy on real wildlife images.

I know this will be successful because our profiling data proves it mathematically. By storing the binary layer weights and activations in 1-bit format, we reduce memory traffic for those layers by a factor of 32. Our roofline analysis shows this eliminates the memory-traffic jam for the chiplet workload, shifting the arithmetic intensity of the binary layers from 12.34 FLOP/byte (the host CPU bottleneck) to 379 FLOP/byte on the chiplet—well into the compute-bound regime. This allows us to hit a target throughput of 1,200 GFLOP/s over a standard 256-bit interface. By resolving this bottleneck, the power draw drops low enough that the camera no longer needs a heat sensor; it can continuously process live video on a solar budget.
